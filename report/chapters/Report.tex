\section{Proposal}

The purpose of this report is to study one analytically transparent fragment of the transformer architecture~\autocite{Vaswani2017}: scaled dot-product attention. The report is intentionally not about full transformer training. Instead, it isolates a single nonlinear operator and examines how two approximations interact:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item a randomized finite-rank compression motivated by randomized numerical linear algebra~\autocite{HalkoMartinssonTropp2011, Mahoney2016};
    \item a mixed-precision realization motivated by modern low-precision training practice~\autocite{MicikeviciusEtAl2018}.
\end{enumerate}

This focus keeps the project aligned with the mathematical language of the lecture notes: dimension reduction, kernel-style matrix constructions, and operator perturbation. It also places the work in the neighborhood of efficient-attention models such as Linformer, Nystr\"omformer, and Performer~\autocite{WangEtAl2020, XiongEtAl2021, ChoromanskiEtAl2021}, while remaining much narrower in scope.

The guiding question is the following.

\begin{idea}[Research question]
    When the attention logits are first compressed by a randomized sketch, can the subsequent computation be carried out in mixed precision with only a lower-order loss of accuracy?
\end{idea}

The intended contribution of the report is modest but precise: a deterministic error decomposition, together with numerical experiments that test whether the finite-precision term is indeed subordinate to the sketching term in relevant parameter regimes.

\section{Operator-Theoretic Setup}

Let $\mathcal{H}_d := \mathbb{R}^d$ and $\mathcal{H}_n := \mathbb{R}^n$, both endowed with their standard Euclidean inner products. We identify matrices in $\mathbb{R}^{n \times d}$ with Hilbert--Schmidt operators
\[
    Q,K,V : \mathcal{H}_d \to \mathcal{H}_n.
\]
Their Hilbert--Schmidt norm is denoted by $\|\cdot\|_{\mathrm{HS}}$, while $\|\cdot\|_2$ denotes the spectral norm on linear operators.

The exact logit operator is
\[
    \mathcal{L}(Q,K) := d^{-1/2} QK^* \in \mathcal{L}(\mathcal{H}_n),
\]
and the rowwise softmax map is denoted by
\[
    \Sigma : \mathbb{R}^{n \times n} \to \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}.
\]
Accordingly, the attention output is the nonlinear operator
\[
    \mathcal{A}(Q,K,V) := \Sigma\!\bigl(\mathcal{L}(Q,K)\bigr)V.
\]

Now let $S \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times s}$ with $s < d$. The associated rank-$s$ positive semidefinite operator on $\mathcal{H}_d$ is
\[
    P_S := SS^*.
\]
The sketched logit operator is then
\[
    \widetilde{\mathcal{L}}_S(Q,K) := d^{-1/2} QP_S K^*,
\]
and the corresponding sketched attention output is
\[
    \widetilde{\mathcal{A}}_S(Q,K,V) := \Sigma\!\bigl(\widetilde{\mathcal{L}}_S(Q,K)\bigr)V.
\]

Thus the basic approximation step is the replacement of the identity on $\mathcal{H}_d$ by the finite-rank operator $P_S$.

\section{Analytical Core}

The analysis begins with a stability property of the rowwise softmax map.

\begin{proposition}[Rowwise softmax is Hilbert--Schmidt Lipschitz]
    Let $L,M \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$. Then
    \[
        \|\Sigma(L) - \Sigma(M)\|_{\mathrm{HS}} \leq \|L - M\|_{\mathrm{HS}}.
    \]
    Consequently, for every $V \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d}$,
    \[
        \|\Sigma(L)V - \Sigma(M)V\|_{\mathrm{HS}} \leq \|L - M\|_{\mathrm{HS}}\,\|V\|_2.
    \]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
    Let $\sigma : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^n$ be the ordinary softmax map on vectors. Its Jacobian at $x \in \mathbb{R}^n$ is
    \[
        D\sigma(x) = \operatorname{Diag}(p) - pp^T,
        \qquad
        p := \sigma(x).
    \]
    For any $z \in \mathbb{R}^n$ one has
    \[
        z^T D\sigma(x) z
        = \sum_{i=1}^n p_i z_i^2 - \Bigl(\sum_{i=1}^n p_i z_i\Bigr)^2
        \leq \sum_{i=1}^n p_i z_i^2
        \leq \|z\|_2^2.
    \]
    Hence $\|D\sigma(x)\|_2 \leq 1$ for all $x$, and the mean value theorem yields
    \[
        \|\sigma(x)-\sigma(y)\|_2 \leq \|x-y\|_2.
    \]
    Applying this estimate row by row gives
    \[
        \|\Sigma(L)-\Sigma(M)\|_{\mathrm{HS}}^2
        = \sum_{i=1}^n \|\sigma(\ell_i)-\sigma(m_i)\|_2^2
        \leq \sum_{i=1}^n \|\ell_i-m_i\|_2^2
        = \|L-M\|_{\mathrm{HS}}^2,
    \]
    where $\ell_i$ and $m_i$ are the rows of $L$ and $M$. The second assertion follows from the submultiplicativity estimate
    \[
        \|XV\|_{\mathrm{HS}} \leq \|X\|_{\mathrm{HS}}\|V\|_2.
    \]
\end{proof}

The next proposition isolates the deterministic sketching error.

\begin{proposition}[Deterministic sketching bound]
    Let $U \subseteq \mathcal{H}_d$ be a subspace containing all row vectors of $Q$ and $K$. Assume that $P_S$ is an $\varepsilon$-approximate identity on $U$, in the sense that
    \[
        \|(I-P_S)u\|_2 \leq \varepsilon \|u\|_2
        \qquad
        \text{for all } u \in U.
    \]
    Then
    \[
        \|\mathcal{L}(Q,K)-\widetilde{\mathcal{L}}_S(Q,K)\|_{\mathrm{HS}}
        \leq
        \frac{\varepsilon}{\sqrt d}\,\|Q\|_{\mathrm{HS}}\,\|K\|_2.
    \]
    Consequently,
    \[
        \|\mathcal{A}(Q,K,V)-\widetilde{\mathcal{A}}_S(Q,K,V)\|_{\mathrm{HS}}
        \leq
        \frac{\varepsilon}{\sqrt d}\,\|Q\|_{\mathrm{HS}}\,\|K\|_2\,\|V\|_2.
    \]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
    Since
    \[
        \mathcal{L}(Q,K)-\widetilde{\mathcal{L}}_S(Q,K)
        = d^{-1/2} Q(I-P_S)K^*,
    \]
    we obtain
    \[
        \|\mathcal{L}(Q,K)-\widetilde{\mathcal{L}}_S(Q,K)\|_{\mathrm{HS}}
        \leq
        \frac{1}{\sqrt d}\,\|Q(I-P_S)\|_{\mathrm{HS}}\,\|K^*\|_2.
    \]
    Every row of $Q$ lies in $U$, so the approximate-identity assumption implies
    \[
        \|Q(I-P_S)\|_{\mathrm{HS}} \leq \varepsilon \|Q\|_{\mathrm{HS}}.
    \]
    Using $\|K^*\|_2 = \|K\|_2$ proves the first estimate. The second follows from the previous proposition.
\end{proof}

This reduces the sketching analysis to a single geometric requirement on the projector $P_S$. In the randomized setting, the role of randNLA is to choose $S$ so that this requirement holds with high probability on a subspace that captures the geometry of the rows of $Q$ and $K$.

The final step is to separate sketching error from finite-precision error.

\begin{proposition}[Error splitting for mixed-precision attention]
    Let $\widehat{\mathcal{L}}_S(Q,K)$ denote the logit matrix produced by a finite-precision implementation of the sketched computation, and define
    \[
        \widehat{\mathcal{A}}_S(Q,K,V)
        := \Sigma\!\bigl(\widehat{\mathcal{L}}_S(Q,K)\bigr)V.
    \]
    Then
    \[
        \|\mathcal{A}(Q,K,V)-\widehat{\mathcal{A}}_S(Q,K,V)\|_{\mathrm{HS}}
        \leq
        \Bigl(
            \|\mathcal{L}(Q,K)-\widetilde{\mathcal{L}}_S(Q,K)\|_{\mathrm{HS}}
            +
            \|\widetilde{\mathcal{L}}_S(Q,K)-\widehat{\mathcal{L}}_S(Q,K)\|_{\mathrm{HS}}
        \Bigr)
        \|V\|_2.
    \]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
    Apply the previous Lipschitz estimate with
    \[
        L = \mathcal{L}(Q,K),
        \qquad
        M = \widehat{\mathcal{L}}_S(Q,K),
    \]
    and then use the triangle inequality
    \[
        \|L-M\|_{\mathrm{HS}}
        \leq
        \|L-\widetilde{\mathcal{L}}_S(Q,K)\|_{\mathrm{HS}}
        +
        \|\widetilde{\mathcal{L}}_S(Q,K)-M\|_{\mathrm{HS}}.
    \]
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Interpretation]
    The first term in the bound is a purely sketching term; the second is a purely numerical term. In the intended mixed-precision regime, the numerical term should be lower order. This is the precise statement that the experiments are meant to test.
\end{remark}

\section{Proposed Experiments}

The numerical part of the project is not meant to be a large benchmark. Its role is to probe the analytical decomposition above. Accordingly, the experiments should compare three levels of computation:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item exact unsketched attention in \texttt{float64};
    \item exact sketched attention in \texttt{float64};
    \item sketched attention in \texttt{float16} and mixed precision.
\end{enumerate}

For each configuration we measure:
\begin{itemize}
    \item the Hilbert--Schmidt error of the logits,
    \item the rowwise simplex error after softmax,
    \item the Hilbert--Schmidt error of the final attention output,
    \item the runtime.
\end{itemize}

The theoretical narrative suggests the following validation strategy. First, compare the exact unsketched and exact sketched logits; this isolates the sketching term. Second, compare the exact sketched and low-precision sketched logits; this isolates the numerical term. Finally, compare the resulting attention outputs. The main hypothesis is that once the sketch dimension $s$ is moderate, the rounding term will be substantially smaller than the sketching term under mixed-precision accumulation, even if naive low precision is noticeably less stable.

\section{Outlook}

The present report deliberately studies only the feature-sketch model $QK^T \approx QSS^TK^T$. This is sufficient for a mathematically clean first analysis. If time permits, the natural extensions are Nystr\"om-type approximations and random-feature approximations of the softmax kernel, which would connect the project more directly with the efficient-attention literature.
