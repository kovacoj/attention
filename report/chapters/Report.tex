\section{Introduction}

The purpose of this report is to study one analytically transparent component of the transformer architecture~\autocite{Vaswani2017}: scaled dot-product attention. Two strands are particularly relevant. The first, rooted in randomized numerical linear algebra~\autocite{HalkoMartinssonTropp2011, Mahoney2016}, replaces the full attention matrix by a sketched surrogate~\autocite{WangEtAl2020, XiongEtAl2021, ChoromanskiEtAl2021}. The second, driven by hardware constraints, carries out the computation in reduced precision~\autocite{MicikeviciusEtAl2018}.

These two approximation strategies are typically studied in isolation. This report asks what happens when they are composed: when the query-key logit computation is approximated by a randomized sketch, can the subsequent computation be carried out in mixed precision with only a lower-order loss of accuracy?

The answer, as we show, is yes---and the mechanism is precise. Sketching reduces the effective dimension of the matrix product from $d$ to $s$, which simultaneously controls the sketching error (via the intrinsic rank of the query--key subspace) and shrinks the finite-precision error (because the inner dimension of the product is smaller). The main result (Theorem~\ref{thm:main}) gives an explicit total error bound and highlights the condition $s \cdot u \ll \varepsilon$ as the leading-order requirement for the finite-precision term to remain lower order when the accompanying norm factors are of comparable scale, where $s$ is the sketch dimension, $u$ is the unit roundoff, and $\varepsilon$ is the sketch tolerance. This condition provides a concrete design rule: sketch dimension and precision should be chosen jointly, not independently.

\section{Operator-Theoretic Setup}

Let $\mathcal{H}_d := \mathbb{R}^d$ and $\mathcal{H}_n := \mathbb{R}^n$, both finite-dimensional Hilbert spaces endowed with their standard Euclidean inner products. We view matrices in $\mathbb{R}^{n \times d}$ as Hilbert--Schmidt operators
\[
    Q,K,V : \mathcal{H}_d \to \mathcal{H}_n.
\]
Their Hilbert--Schmidt norm is denoted by $\|\cdot\|_{\mathrm{HS}}$, while $\|\cdot\|_2$ denotes the spectral norm on linear operators.

The exact scaled logit matrix, viewed as an operator on $\mathcal{H}_n$, is
\[
    \mathcal{L}(Q,K) := d^{-1/2} QK^* \in \mathcal{L}(\mathcal{H}_n),
\]
and the rowwise softmax map is denoted by
\[
    \Sigma : \mathbb{R}^{n \times n} \to \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}.
\]
Accordingly, the attention output is the nonlinear operator
\[
    \mathcal{A}(Q,K,V) := \Sigma\!\bigl(\mathcal{L}(Q,K)\bigr)V.
\]

Now let $S \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times s}$ with $s < d$. The sketched logit operator is
\[
    \widetilde{\mathcal{L}}_S(Q,K) := d^{-1/2} QSS^*K^*.
\]
The operator $P_S := SS^*$ is positive semidefinite with rank at most $s$, but it is not in general an orthogonal projector unless the columns of $S$ are orthonormal. The corresponding sketched attention output is
\[
    \widetilde{\mathcal{A}}_S(Q,K,V) := \Sigma\!\bigl(\widetilde{\mathcal{L}}_S(Q,K)\bigr)V.
\]

Thus the basic approximation step is the replacement of inner products $\langle q_i, k_j \rangle$ by sketched inner products $\langle S^* q_i, S^* k_j \rangle$.

\section{Analytical Core}

The analysis begins with a stability property of the rowwise softmax map.

\begin{proposition}[Rowwise softmax is Hilbert--Schmidt Lipschitz]
    Let $L,M \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$. Then
    \[
        \|\Sigma(L) - \Sigma(M)\|_{\mathrm{HS}} \leq \|L - M\|_{\mathrm{HS}}.
    \]
    Consequently, for every $V \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d}$,
    \[
        \|\Sigma(L)V - \Sigma(M)V\|_{\mathrm{HS}} \leq \|L - M\|_{\mathrm{HS}}\,\|V\|_2.
    \]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
    Let $\sigma : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^n$ be the softmax map on vectors with Jacobian $D\sigma(x) = \operatorname{Diag}(p) - pp^T$ where $p = \sigma(x)$. For any $z \in \mathbb{R}^n$, $z^T D\sigma(x)\,z = \sum_i p_i z_i^2 - (\sum_i p_i z_i)^2 \leq \|z\|_2^2$, so $\|D\sigma(x)\|_2 \leq 1$ for all $x$. The mean value theorem gives $\|\sigma(x)-\sigma(y)\|_2 \leq \|x-y\|_2$; applying this row by row and squaring yields the HS bound. The second assertion follows from $\|XV\|_{\mathrm{HS}} \leq \|X\|_{\mathrm{HS}}\|V\|_2$.
\end{proof}

The next proposition isolates the deterministic sketching error. Since attention logits are bilinear forms $q_i^* k_j$, the relevant notion is that $S$ approximately preserves inner products on the row space of $Q$ and $K$.

\begin{proposition}[Deterministic sketching bound]
    \label{prop:deterministic}
    Let $U \subseteq \mathcal{H}_d$ be the joint row space of $Q$ and $K$, equivalently the subspace spanned by the columns of $[Q^T \;\; K^T]$, and let $r := \dim U$. Assume that $S$ is an $\varepsilon$-subspace embedding for $U$, meaning
    \[
        \bigl|\langle S^*u, S^*v\rangle - \langle u, v\rangle\bigr| \leq \varepsilon\,\|u\|_2\,\|v\|_2
        \qquad
        \text{for all } u, v \in U.
    \]
    Equivalently, if $U_0 \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times r}$ is any orthonormal basis matrix for $U$, then $\|U_0^T SS^* U_0 - I_r\|_2 \leq \varepsilon$. Then
    \[
        \|\mathcal{L}(Q,K)-\widetilde{\mathcal{L}}_S(Q,K)\|_{\mathrm{HS}}
        \leq
        \frac{\varepsilon}{\sqrt d}\,\|Q\|_{\mathrm{HS}}\,\|K\|_2.
    \]
    Consequently,
    \[
        \|\mathcal{A}(Q,K,V)-\widetilde{\mathcal{A}}_S(Q,K,V)\|_{\mathrm{HS}}
        \leq
        \frac{\varepsilon}{\sqrt d}\,\|Q\|_{\mathrm{HS}}\,\|K\|_2\,\|V\|_2.
    \]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
    Let $U_0 \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times r}$ have orthonormal columns spanning the joint row space of $Q$ and $K$. Write $Q = Q_U U_0^T$ and $K = K_U U_0^T$, where $Q_U = QU_0 \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times r}$ and $K_U = KU_0 \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times r}$. Then
    \[
        QK^* - QSS^*K^*
        = Q_U\bigl(I_r - U_0^T SS^* U_0\bigr)K_U^T.
    \]
    The subspace-embedding condition is exactly $\|U_0^T SS^* U_0 - I_r\|_2 \leq \varepsilon$, so
    \[
        \|QK^* - QSS^*K^*\|_{\mathrm{HS}}
        \leq \|Q_U\|_{\mathrm{HS}}\,\|I_r - U_0^T SS^* U_0\|_2\,\|K_U^T\|_2
        \leq \varepsilon\,\|Q\|_{\mathrm{HS}}\,\|K\|_2,
    \]
    where we used $\|Q_U\|_{\mathrm{HS}} = \|Q\|_{\mathrm{HS}}$ and $\|K_U\|_2 = \|K\|_2$. Multiplying by $d^{-1/2}$ gives the logit bound. The attention-output bound follows from Proposition~1.
\end{proof}

The proposition should be read as a conditional stability statement: it separates the attention-stability argument from the probabilistic question of when the sketch approximately preserves inner products on the relevant subspace.

The next result gives this probabilistic guarantee for Gaussian sketches. A scaled Gaussian matrix $S \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times s}$ with entries $\mathcal{N}(0, 1/s)$ is a subspace embedding for any fixed $r$-dimensional subspace with high probability, requiring $s = O(r/\varepsilon^2)$---not $O(d/\varepsilon^2)$.

\begin{proposition}[Probabilistic subspace embedding guarantee]
    \label{prop:probabilistic}
    Let $U \subseteq \mathcal{H}_d$ be an $r$-dimensional subspace. Let $S \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times s}$ have i.i.d.\ entries $S_{ij} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 1/s)$. For $\varepsilon \in (0, 1)$ and $\delta \in (0, 1)$, if
    \[
        s \;\geq\; C\,\frac{r + \ln(1/\delta)}{\varepsilon^2},
    \]
    for a universal constant $C$, then with probability at least $1 - \delta$, $S$ is an $\varepsilon$-subspace embedding for $U$. If $U$ contains the joint row space of $Q$ and $K$, then with the same probability,
    \[
        \|\mathcal{A}(Q,K,V)-\widetilde{\mathcal{A}}_S(Q,K,V)\|_{\mathrm{HS}}
        \leq
        \frac{\varepsilon}{\sqrt d}\,\|Q\|_{\mathrm{HS}}\,\|K\|_2\,\|V\|_2.
    \]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
    Let $U_0 \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times r}$ be a matrix whose columns form an orthonormal basis for $U$. Then $S$ is an $\varepsilon$-subspace embedding for $U$ if and only if $\|U_0^T SS^* U_0 - I_r\|_2 \leq \varepsilon$. The matrix $U_0^T SS^* U_0 = (S^* U_0)^*(S^* U_0)$ has the same distribution as $G^*G/s$ where $G \in \mathbb{R}^{s \times r}$ has i.i.d.\ standard normal entries. Standard JL concentration~\autocite{HalkoMartinssonTropp2011} gives $\Pr(\|G^*G/s - I_r\|_2 > \varepsilon) \leq 2\exp(-c\,s\varepsilon^2 + r)$ for a universal constant $c > 0$, provided $s$ is large enough relative to $r/\varepsilon^2$. The stated condition with an appropriate $C$ ensures the failure probability is at most $\delta$. The attention error bound follows from Proposition~\ref{prop:deterministic}.
\end{proof}

The key feature is the dependence on $r$ rather than $d$: when the query--key rows lie near a low-dimensional subspace, a much smaller sketch suffices.

We now turn to the finite-precision error. The standard Higham-type forward-error analysis~\autocite{Higham2002} for the matrix product $AB$ with $A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times s}$, $B \in \mathbb{R}^{s \times n}$ gives
\[
    |\widehat{AB} - AB| \leq \gamma_s\, |A|\,|B|,
    \qquad
    \gamma_s := \frac{su}{1 - su},
\]
where $u$ is the unit roundoff. For the sketched logit product $d^{-1/2}(QS)(KS)^*$, the inner dimension is $s$, so the finite-precision error is proportional to $s \cdot u$---not $d \cdot u$ as it would be for the full product $QK^*$.

\begin{proposition}[Finite-precision error for sketched logits]
    \label{prop:rounding}
    Let $\widehat{\mathcal{L}}_S(Q,K)$ denote the logit matrix computed in floating-point arithmetic with unit roundoff $u$, and let $\gamma_s := su/(1-su)$ for $su < 1$. Then
    \[
        \|\widetilde{\mathcal{L}}_S(Q,K) - \widehat{\mathcal{L}}_S(Q,K)\|_{\mathrm{HS}}
        \leq
        \frac{\gamma_s\,\|QS\|_{\mathrm{HS}}\,\|KS\|_{\mathrm{HS}}}{\sqrt{d}}.
    \]
    Consequently,
    \[
        \|\widetilde{\mathcal{A}}_S(Q,K,V) - \widehat{\mathcal{A}}_S(Q,K,V)\|_{\mathrm{HS}}
        \leq
        \frac{\gamma_s\,\|QS\|_{\mathrm{HS}}\,\|KS\|_{\mathrm{HS}}\,\|V\|_2}{\sqrt{d}}.
    \]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
    Write the sketched logit product as $d^{-1/2} A B^*$ with $A = QS \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times s}$ and $B = KS \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times s}$. The standard forward-error bound for matrix multiplication~\autocite{Higham2002} gives
    \[
        |\widehat{AB}^* - AB^*| \leq \gamma_s\, |A|\,|B^*|,
    \]
    entrywise in absolute value. Taking the Frobenius (Hilbert--Schmidt) norm and applying the Cauchy--Schwarz inequality for matrices,
    \[
        \|\widehat{AB}^* - AB^*\|_{\mathrm{HS}}
        \leq \gamma_s\, \||A|\,|B^*|\|_{\mathrm{HS}}
        \leq \gamma_s\, \|A\|_{\mathrm{HS}}\,\|B\|_{\mathrm{HS}}.
    \]
    Multiplying by $d^{-1/2}$ yields the logit bound. The attention-output bound follows from Proposition~1.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Scope of the finite-precision bound]
    The bound in Proposition~\ref{prop:rounding} models the error in the final sketched logit product $(QS)(KS)^*$, assuming that $QS$ and $KS$ are given exactly. The experiments measure a broader implementation error: $Q$, $K$, $V$, and $S$ are first quantized to the storage dtype, and the sketched factors $QS$ and $KS$ are then formed from those quantized tensors before the final logit product. Thus the measured $E_{\mathrm{fp}}$ includes storage quantization and low-precision projection effects in addition to the multiplication-only term analyzed in the proposition.
\end{remark}

The factor $\gamma_s$ scales linearly with $s$: compared to an unsketched product with inner dimension $d$, sketching changes the leading roundoff coefficient from $O(d \cdot u)$ to $O(s \cdot u)$. This is the formal statement of the intuition that ``compressing first makes low precision safer.''
Combining the probabilistic subspace embedding guarantee with the finite-precision error analysis yields the main result.

\begin{theorem}[Mixed-precision sketched attention]
    \label{thm:main}
    Under the hypotheses of Propositions~\ref{prop:probabilistic} and~\ref{prop:rounding}, with probability at least $1-\delta$,
    \[
        \|\mathcal{A}(Q,K,V) - \widehat{\mathcal{A}}_S(Q,K,V)\|_{\mathrm{HS}}
        \leq
        \frac{1}{\sqrt{d}}
        \Bigl(
            \varepsilon\, \|Q\|_{\mathrm{HS}}\,\|K\|_2
            +
            \gamma_s\, \|QS\|_{\mathrm{HS}}\,\|KS\|_{\mathrm{HS}}
        \Bigr)
        \|V\|_2.
    \]
    The first term is the sketching error; the second is the finite-precision error.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
    By Proposition~1 and the triangle inequality,
    \[
        \|\mathcal{A} - \widehat{\mathcal{A}}_S\|_{\mathrm{HS}}
        \leq
        \bigl(\|\mathcal{L} - \widetilde{\mathcal{L}}_S\|_{\mathrm{HS}} + \|\widetilde{\mathcal{L}}_S - \widehat{\mathcal{L}}_S\|_{\mathrm{HS}}\bigr)\,\|V\|_2.
    \]
    Apply Proposition~\ref{prop:deterministic} to the first term and Proposition~\ref{prop:rounding} to the second.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Lower-order regime]
    Theorem~\ref{thm:main} isolates the roundoff coefficient $\gamma_s = O(s \cdot u)$ explicitly. If, in addition, the norm factors satisfy a scale comparison such as
    \[
        \|QS\|_{\mathrm{HS}}\,\|KS\|_{\mathrm{HS}} = O\!\bigl(\|Q\|_{\mathrm{HS}}\,\|K\|_2\bigr),
    \]
    then $s \cdot u \ll \varepsilon$ is the leading-order requirement for the finite-precision term to remain lower order than the sketching term.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Design rule]
    Theorem~\ref{thm:main} yields a concrete joint design rule. For float16 ($u_{16} \approx 5 \times 10^{-4}$) and typical sketch dimensions $s \leq 64$, we have $s \cdot u_{16} \leq 0.032$, which is smaller than typical sketch tolerances $\varepsilon \geq 0.1$. For float32 ($u_{32} \approx 6 \times 10^{-8}$), the condition is essentially always satisfied. For float8 E4M3 ($u_{\text{fp8}} \approx 0.0625$), even $s = 16$ gives $s \cdot u_{\text{fp8}} \approx 1.0$, which is comparable to $\varepsilon$---so the finite-precision term is no longer clearly lower order. The rule also reveals a regime to avoid: if $s$ is chosen too large relative to $1/u$, the finite-precision error can overtake the sketching error, defeating the purpose of mixed precision.
\end{remark}

\section{Numerical Experiments}

The experiments probe the decomposition of Theorem~\ref{thm:main} directly. For each combination of parameters $(n, d, s)$ and each precision configuration we compute three logit matrices:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item $\mathcal{L}(Q,K)$: exact unsketched logits in \texttt{float64} (reference);
    \item $\widetilde{\mathcal{L}}_S(Q,K)$: exact sketched logits in \texttt{float64};
    \item $\widehat{\mathcal{L}}_S(Q,K)$: sketched logits in the target precision.
\end{enumerate}
The sketching error is $E_{\mathrm{sk}} := \|\mathcal{L} - \widetilde{\mathcal{L}}_S\|_{\mathrm{HS}}$ and the finite-precision error is $E_{\mathrm{fp}} := \|\widetilde{\mathcal{L}}_S - \widehat{\mathcal{L}}_S\|_{\mathrm{HS}}$. The total error is $E_{\mathrm{tot}} := \|\mathcal{L} - \widehat{\mathcal{L}}_S\|_{\mathrm{HS}}$. The same decomposition is recorded for the attention output. We test four precision configurations: \texttt{fp32} (all stages in float32), \texttt{full\_fp16} (storage, projection, final logit product, and softmax in float16), \texttt{mixed\_fp16} (storage and projection in float16, followed by a float32 final logit product and float32 softmax), and \texttt{fp8\_store} (storage and projection in float8 E4M3, followed by a float32 final logit product and float32 softmax).

The sketch $S \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times s}$ is a scaled Gaussian matrix with i.i.d.\ $\mathcal{N}(0, 1/s)$ entries; the scaling is chosen so that $\mathbb{E}[SS^*] = I_d$. We sweep $n \in \{64, 128, 256\}$, $d \in \{32, 64\}$, $s \in \{8, 16, 24, 32\}$, and average over three seeds. Data and sketch seeds are independent. The full numerical results are available in the committed CSV file \texttt{results/attention\_sweep.csv}.

Since the synthetic $Q$ and $K$ are i.i.d.\ Gaussian, their joint row space is typically full-dimensional, so for $d = 64$ and $s \leq 32$ the subspace-embedding regime from Proposition~\ref{prop:probabilistic} is not expected to hold. The experiment should therefore be read as an intentionally under-sketched stress test of the error decomposition, not as evidence that such small feature sketches preserve attention accurately.

Three observations stand out. First, for fp32 and fp16, $E_{\mathrm{sk}} / E_{\mathrm{fp}}$ is consistently large ($10^3$--$10^6$), confirming that the dominant error source is the randomized sketch. The relative sketching error exceeds 140\%, so this regime is not a good attention approximation; rather, it is a deliberate stress test in which the sketching floor is already very high, while the relative finite-precision error stays below 0.16\%. Second, \texttt{full\_fp16} and \texttt{mixed\_fp16} have finite-precision errors of the same order, consistent with Proposition~\ref{prop:rounding}: storage quantization (the $s \cdot u$ factor) dominates any benefit from higher-precision accumulation. Third, fp8 storage substantially increases the finite-precision error relative to fp16 (from $\sim$0.3 to $\sim$30 in absolute terms, or from $\sim$0.1\% to $\sim$10\% relative), but the sketching error still dominates by a factor of $\sim$14--15. That is, fp8 storage degrades accuracy noticeably compared to fp16, yet in the tested synthetic regime the total error remains sketching-dominated. A regime where finite precision dominates would require even lower precision or larger $s$; the current experiments do not reach it. Thus the results support the error decomposition and illustrate the degradation predicted by Theorem~\ref{thm:main} for increasing $s \cdot u$, but they do not demonstrate a high-quality efficient-attention approximation or a full inversion of the error ratio.

\section{Outlook}

The present report studies only the feature-sketch model $QK^* \approx QSS^*K^*$. This is analytically transparent, but it is not the strongest empirical setting for efficient attention: it compresses the model dimension $d$, whereas the main computational bottleneck in long-sequence transformers is usually the sequence dimension $n$, and on generic Gaussian data the relevant row space is essentially full-dimensional. The most promising next step is therefore to move from feature sketching to random-feature approximation of the softmax kernel, following Performer-style constructions~\autocite{ChoromanskiEtAl2021}. In that setting one approximates
\[
    \exp\!\bigl(q^*k / \sqrt d\bigr) \approx \phi(q)^*\phi(k),
\]
which leads to a kernelized attention surrogate of the form
\[
    \widetilde{A}(Q,K,V) = \widetilde{D}^{-1}\Phi(Q)\Phi(K)^*V.
\]
This would support a more compelling error decomposition,
\[
    \|A - \widehat{A}\| \leq \|A - \widetilde{A}\| + \|\widetilde{A} - \widehat{A}\|,
\]
where the first term is the random-feature approximation error and the second is the additional finite-precision error. A natural design rule would then compare the random-feature dimension $m$ with the unit roundoff $u$ through a condition of the form $m u \ll \varepsilon_{\mathrm{rf}}$, where $\varepsilon_{\mathrm{rf}}$ is the random-feature error floor.

\subsection{Random-Feature Prototype}

The repository now contains a prototype implementation of this pivot. It uses a positive orthogonal random-feature map modeled on Performer-style constructions: the projection matrix is assembled from block-orthogonal Gaussian factors, and the exponential feature map is stabilized by subtracting a querywise maximum for queries and a global maximum for keys before exponentiation. This keeps the map positive while reducing overflow risk in finite precision.

The prototype evaluates the decomposition
\[
    \|A - \widehat{A}\| \leq \|A - \widetilde{A}\| + \|\widetilde{A} - \widehat{A}\|,
\]
at three levels: the unnormalized softmax kernel, the normalized attention weights, and the final attention output. Here $A$ denotes exact softmax attention, $\widetilde{A}$ is the same random-feature approximation evaluated in \texttt{float64}, and $\widehat{A}$ is the corresponding low-precision implementation. The first term is therefore the intrinsic random-feature approximation floor, while the second isolates the additional precision loss for a fixed feature map.

To make the experimental regimes more informative than the current full-rank Gaussian feature-sketch test, the prototype supports three input families: (i) i.i.d.\ Gaussian $Q,K,V$ as a baseline, (ii) low-rank synthetic activations of the form $Q = ZA + \sigma N_Q$, $K = ZB + \sigma N_K$, and (iii) query, key, and value tensors extracted from a selected head of a pretrained transformer layer on real text. This creates a direct path from mathematically controlled synthetic models to structured activations arising in an actual language model.

That pivot allows experiments on more realistic data while keeping the mixed-precision question central. Other possible extensions are Nystr\"om-type approximations~\autocite{XiongEtAl2021}, structured random matrices in place of Gaussian sketches, and an operator-theoretic treatment for Hilbert--Schmidt limits under compactness or spectral-decay assumptions.
